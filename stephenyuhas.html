The Operational Excellence Formula

Five imperatives.
One destination: Flow.

Every engagement I run is organized around these five imperatives — in sequence, because each one builds on the last.

01

Engineer a Process That Captures the Data You Need Without Slowing the Doers Down

The foundation. Start with a data model. Augment enterprise systems. Never put the overhead on the people doing the actual work.

Repeatability
02

Build Trust & Transparency by Making Data Available to Everyone in a Way They Can Digest

Broadcast the data. Don't hide dirty laundry. Nothing is more powerful than showing vulnerability AND a plan to address it.

Trust
03

Use the Data to Make Decisions, Predict the Future, and Make Improvements

Once people see and react to the data, using it becomes easy. What might happen? What can we do? What will we do if it does?

Predictability
04

Give Everyone a Voice Through Guided Retrospection

No titles. No hierarchy. Vegas rules. Pareto-driven. The three most powerful questions: Why? So what? What can we do?

Retrospection
05

Implement and Measure Improvements. Celebrate Success AND Failures.

Do what you committed to. When something doesn't work, abandon it quickly, document the learning, and celebrate it. Failures are data.

Improvement

The Rules

26 rules. One story.
30 years of pattern recognition.

Every rule in this framework came from a real engagement — a real team, a real failure, a real breakthrough. Here are the first five. The rest are in the book.

Rule 01
Stack the Dishes Before You Build Anything
Clear the chaos before you build infrastructure. You cannot build a repeatable process on top of an unmanaged backlog.
Rule 02
Stop the Bleeding First
Get the team breathing again before you fix the system. Triage first. Build the infrastructure underneath them while they stabilize.
Rule 03
No Double Entry. Ever.
Double entry is a tax on doers and a source of data integrity failure. One system of record. Augment alongside it. Never duplicate.
Rule 04
Don't Size in Points. Size in Time.
Story points answer how complex. Your stakeholders need to know when. Those are different questions. Use a model that answers the right one.
Rule 05
Sprint Capacity Is Defined by the Team, Not Imposed on Them
Don't do this in a vacuum. The team needs to own the number — so they have to have a voice in defining it. They will defend what they own.
Rule 13
Servant Leadership Is Not Optional
Facilitators are there to serve the doers. The moment a facilitator becomes an overhead creator rather than an overhead remover, the process begins to fail.

26 Rules. All in the book.

Getting From Chaos to Flow captures all 26 rules — each with the story behind it, the team that taught it, and the human moment that proved it worked.
